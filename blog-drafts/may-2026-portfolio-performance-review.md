May was a broad, strong month across the board. Of the 74 portfolios PortfolioDB tracks, 71 finished positive, and the average return across all of them landed at +2.27%. That's the kind of month where almost everything works, which makes the differences between strategies more interesting rather than less. When nearly everyone is up, the spread between the portfolio that returned +7.2% and the one that lost -5.2% tells you more about how a strategy is actually built than an average month would.

## What drove the month

A few things lined up at once. The Federal Reserve held rates steady at its late-April meeting for a third straight time, and the accompanying signals were widely read as confirmation that the tightening cycle is over and the next move from here is more likely to be a cut than a hike. That shift in expectations pulled the 10-year Treasury yield down toward 4.5%, which made stocks look more attractive relative to bonds almost overnight. On top of that, first-quarter earnings season ran hot, with roughly four out of five reporting companies beating analyst estimates, and core inflation came in cooler than expected. Technology and AI-related names led the charge, with the Nasdaq posting one of its strongest months in recent memory and the S&P 500 closing May at a record high. Put plainly: the market spent May convincing itself that the rate-hike era is behind us, and it priced that relief in fast.

Whatever the cause, equities clearly led. The [US Stock Market portfolio](/portfolios/us-stock-market) (100% US total market exposure) returned +5.18% for the month, well ahead of the [US 60/40 Portfolio](/portfolios/united-states-60-40-portfolio)'s +3.15% and the [Global Stock Market portfolio](/portfolios/global-stock-market)'s +4.58%. That roughly two-percentage-point gap between a 100% stock allocation and a 60/40 blend is a clean illustration of what bonds are actually for in a portfolio. They didn't add much in a month like May, but they're the reason a 60/40 portfolio's worst months look nothing like its best ones.

The breadth of the month is just as telling as the average. Ninety-six percent of tracked portfolios finished positive, regardless of whether they hold stocks, bonds, commodities, or some tactical blend of all three. Months like that don't come around often, and they're a reminder that diversification is a tool for the months that *don't* look like May, not the ones that do. A portfolio's behavior in a broad rally tells you very little about how it will hold up when the rally ends; what matters is what it's built to do when conditions reverse.

## The winners: momentum strategies caught the wave

Two portfolios tied for the top spot in May, both up +7.20%: [GEM + Emerging Markets Dual Momentum](/portfolios/gem-emerging-markets-dual-momentum) and [Vigilant Asset Allocation - G4 Aggressive](/portfolios/vigilant-asset-allocation-g4-aggressive). Both are momentum-driven strategies that rotate into whichever asset class has shown the strongest recent trend, and in a month where equities broadly rallied, that mechanism pointed them squarely at the strongest movers.

Right behind them, [GEM Dual Momentum](/portfolios/gem-dual-momentum) and [Paired Switching by Lewis Glenn](/portfolios/paired-switching-lewis-glenn) each returned +5.26%, essentially matching the US Stock Market portfolio's +5.18%. That's worth pausing on: these are tactical strategies designed to rotate *away* from equities when conditions look weak, yet in May they captured close to the full upside of a straight stock allocation anyway. A momentum strategy that can keep pace with the market on the way up, while still being built to step aside on the way down, is doing exactly what it's supposed to do.

A few buy-and-hold strategies also had standout months for a more straightforward reason: heavy equity weightings. The [Warren Buffett Portfolio](/portfolios/warren-buffett-portfolio) returned +4.74% and the [Ben Felix Model Portfolio](/portfolios/ben-felix-model-portfolio) returned +4.62%. Neither holds any bonds, and that positioning paid off directly when stocks were the place to be.

## The laggard: when trend-following gets the timing wrong

One portfolio stood well apart from the pack on the downside. [Ivy Portfolio - Rotation](/portfolios/ivy-portfolio-rotation) lost -5.21% in May, the only portfolio among all 74 tracked to post a meaningfully negative month. Everything else near the bottom was close to flat by comparison: [Adaptive Asset Allocation](/portfolios/adaptive-asset-allocation) finished at -1.44%, [Quint Switching Filtered](/portfolios/quint-switching-filtered) at -0.02%, and [Composite Dual Momentum](/portfolios/composite-dual-momentum) at +0.05%, technically positive but negligible.

Ivy Rotation is a trend-following strategy that picks from a small basket of asset classes based on momentum signals calculated at the end of the prior month. When a market reverses direction quickly, a strategy built this way can end up positioned for *last* month's trend just as a new one takes hold. That's the textbook definition of getting whipsawed, and it isn't a flaw unique to Ivy Rotation. It's the structural tradeoff every trend-following strategy makes in exchange for sidestepping the long, grinding downturns that buy-and-hold investors have to sit through. Most months, that tradeoff works in the strategy's favor. May looks like one of the months where it didn't.

By contrast, the [Conservative Income Portfolio by Schwab](/portfolios/conservative-income-portfolio-schwab) and its tax-aware counterpart each landed around +0.26% for the month. Neither is designed to chase a rally; both are built to preserve capital and produce steady income, and that's exactly what a quiet, low-volatility month from them looks like. A near-flat result from a conservative income strategy in a month when the broad market was up 5% isn't underperformance in any meaningful sense. It's the strategy doing precisely what an investor who chose it should expect.

## Buy and Hold vs. Tactical: a narrow gap, not a verdict

Across the 44 Buy and Hold portfolios in the database, the average May return was +2.37%. Across the 28 Tactical portfolios, it was +2.07%, a gap of about three-tenths of a percentage point. The two Robo-Advisor portfolios averaged +3.13%.

Our take: a 0.3 percentage point gap between two categories, each made up of dozens of very different strategies, isn't a finding. It's noise, and treating it as anything more would be a mistake we'd be making right alongside the reader. Buy and Hold "won" May by a margin that would flip with a single different rounding convention, and that's exactly the point: a month this calm and this broadly positive is the *least* informative environment for telling these two approaches apart. Tactical strategies don't exist to keep pace in months like May. They exist for the months that look nothing like it, the ones where a market gives back 20% over a few weeks and a rules-based system either sidesteps part of that or it doesn't.

So the comparison worth making isn't this month's average. It's the one you can already run on this site: pull up a Buy and Hold portfolio and a Tactical one with similar risk profiles side by side in the [Portfolio Comparison tool](/compare), and look past the recent return to the max drawdown and the Ulcer Index. That's where the two philosophies actually diverge, and it's where a single calm month like May has nothing useful to say either way.

What May *can* tell you is whether each individual strategy behaved the way its own design would predict, and on that front, the month was fairly clean. Momentum strategies built to chase strength did exactly that. Equity-heavy buy-and-hold portfolios captured the rally in proportion to their stock weighting. Defensive and bond-heavy strategies mostly sat close to flat, which is precisely their job in a month like this one. That's a much more useful pattern to notice than which broad category happened to land 0.3 points ahead.
