The Bogleheads Three-Fund Portfolio holds three index funds. The Four-Fund version holds four. Backtested since the early 1990s, the two have produced almost identical results, and the place where they actually diverge isn't where most people expect.

## What the Fourth Fund Actually Is

The [Bogleheads Three-Fund Portfolio](/portfolios/bogleheads-three-fund-portfolio) splits its allocation three ways: 50% US total stock market, 30% international developed equity, and 20% in a US aggregate bond fund. It's the textbook version of the Boglehead philosophy: own the whole market across a handful of cheap, broad funds and leave it alone.

The [Bogleheads Four-Fund Portfolio](/portfolios/bogleheads-four-fund-portfolio) keeps the same 50/30 stock split but carves the 20% bond allocation into two pieces: 10% in the same aggregate bond fund and 10% in Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS). That's the entire difference. The stock side is untouched. The only thing that changes is what half of the bond sleeve is made of.

This is worth flagging because "four-fund portfolio" gets used loosely online. Some versions add international bonds, others add a dedicated real estate slice. This particular version is a bond-sleeve modification aimed at one thing: inflation protection.

## The Numbers, Side by Side

Over more than three decades of backtested history, the two portfolios are close enough to be a rounding error in most categories:

| Stat | Three-Fund | Four-Fund |
|---|---|---|
| CAGR | 9.36% | 9.29% |
| Max Drawdown | -43.28% | -43.72% |
| Sharpe Ratio | 0.44 | 0.43 |
| Sortino Ratio | 0.06 | 0.06 |
| Best Year | +28.13% | +27.53% |
| Worst Year | -29.22% | -30.00% |
| 10-Year CAGR | 10.82% | 10.91% |

On almost every long-run metric, the Four-Fund version is a hair behind: slightly lower CAGR, a slightly deeper max drawdown, a slightly worse worst year. The one place it pulls ahead is the trailing 10-year CAGR, where it edges out the Three-Fund by about a tenth of a point. That gap is small, but it isn't random, and it points to exactly when the TIPS sleeve does its job.

## Where TIPS Helped, and Where It Didn't

Rolling 10-year returns tell a more complete story than a single CAGR number. For the Three-Fund Portfolio, rolling 10-year returns ranged from a low of 0.25% to a high of 14.60%, averaging 7.77%. For the Four-Fund Portfolio, the range was 0.09% to 14.35%, averaging 7.71%. In other words, across the full set of 10-year windows in the backtest, the Four-Fund version had both a lower floor and a lower ceiling than the Three-Fund version, despite its better most-recent 10-year number.

The crisis periods tell a similar story. During the dot-com crash (2000-2002), the Three-Fund Portfolio returned -8.32% annualized versus -8.66% for the Four-Fund. During the 2007-2009 financial crisis, it was -2.30% versus -2.35%. In both cases, the TIPS sleeve was a slight drag rather than a cushion. That's not a fluke: TIPS prices can fall sharply during deflationary shocks, when real yields spike even as headline inflation falls, which is close to what happened in both 2008 and the early 2000s.

So why does the Four-Fund version pull ahead on the most recent 10-year window? The most likely driver is the 2021-2023 inflation surge. TIPS principal adjusts upward with CPI, and that adjustment showed up directly in the Four-Fund's bond sleeve during a stretch when nominal bond funds were getting hit by rising rates with no inflation offset to compensate. It's a useful illustration of what TIPS are actually for: they don't make a portfolio better in general, they shift when it performs better and when it performs worse. The same mechanism that helped over the most recent decade was a small drag during the deflationary shocks of 2000-2002 and 2008-2009.

## How Both Compare to a Simple 60/40

It's worth putting both portfolios next to the [US 60/40 Portfolio](/portfolios/united-states-60-40-portfolio), which has a much longer track record (over 56 years) and a more conservative 60% stock allocation. The 60/40 has returned 9.54% annualized with a max drawdown of -29.68%, a Sharpe ratio of 0.51, and an Ulcer Performance Index of 0.84.

Both Bogleheads portfolios have kept pace with or slightly trailed the 60/40 on raw CAGR, but with drawdowns about 14 percentage points deeper (-43% versus -30%) and a noticeably lower Sharpe ratio and Ulcer Performance Index. That's the cost of running an 80% equity allocation instead of 60%: more upside in good years (the Three-Fund's best year of +28.13% beats the 60/40's worst year by a wide margin in the other direction too), but a much rougher ride when stocks and bonds both struggle.

## Which One Should You Actually Use?

If you're choosing between these two, the honest answer is that it mostly doesn't matter. The Four-Fund Portfolio isn't meaningfully more diversified in a way that shows up in the numbers. It's the same 80/20 stock-to-bond portfolio with half the bond sleeve swapped for an inflation hedge. Over more than 30 years, that swap has been close to a wash, helping a little in the most recent decade and costing a little in the two worst crises in the dataset.

Where the Four-Fund version makes sense is if you have a specific view on inflation risk and want a built-in hedge without adding a fifth fund or rebalancing band. Where the Three-Fund version makes sense is, frankly, almost everywhere else: it's one fund simpler, performs essentially the same over the long run, and is the version most commonly cited in the [Bogleheads community](https://www.bogleheads.org/wiki/Three-fund_portfolio) and in *The Bogleheads' Guide to Investing*.

In practice, the choice between these two probably matters less than which broker you use, how low your expense ratios are, and whether you actually stick with an 80% equity allocation through a stretch like 2000-2009. The TIPS sleeve is a reasonable pick if inflation risk specifically is what keeps you up at night. For everyone else, the Three-Fund version is one less fund to think about and gives up essentially nothing for it.
