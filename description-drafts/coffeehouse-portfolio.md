The **Coffeehouse Portfolio** was created by Bill Schultheis, a former stockbroker turned financial advisor who introduced it in his 2002 book [*The Coffeehouse Investor: How to Build Wealth, Ignore Wall Street, and Get on with Your Life*](https://www.amazon.com/Coffeehouse-Investor-Build-Ignore-Street/dp/1591840724). Schultheis designed the portfolio as a direct rebuke of the stock-picking and market-timing promoted by the financial industry, arguing that simplicity and discipline beat sophistication over time. The portfolio allocates half its assets to a total bond market index and splits the remaining half equally across seven equity index funds spanning domestic and international markets.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nSchultheis built his approach on three principles: save consistently, diversify broadly, and stop trying to beat the market. The equal-weighted equity sleeve covers large-cap blend, large-cap value, small-cap blend, small-cap value, international developed, real estate, and a broad US market index. This structure creates a natural tilt toward value and small-cap stocks -- factors supported by decades of academic research -- without requiring active management. The fixed 50% bond allocation is designed to reduce volatility and encourage disciplined rebalancing when markets move.\n\n## Who It's For\nThis portfolio suits investors who want a simple, set-and-forget approach with genuine diversification across equity styles and asset classes. The heavy bond allocation makes it appropriate for conservative to moderate investors, and the overall simplicity makes it well-suited for those new to index investing or uncomfortable with the swings of an all-equity portfolio.\n\n## Pros\n- Simple structure with just eight funds covers a wide range of equity factors and fixed income\n- Equal weighting across seven equity categories creates a structural tilt toward value and small-cap\n- 50% bond allocation provides meaningful downside protection in equity bear markets\n- Low-cost index approach minimizes fees over the long run\n\n## Cons\n- 50% bond allocation significantly limits growth potential for investors with long time horizons\n- Equal weighting across seven equity categories creates a structural underweight to large-cap growth stocks\n- Rebalancing across eight funds can generate more tax events in taxable accounts than simpler portfolios
