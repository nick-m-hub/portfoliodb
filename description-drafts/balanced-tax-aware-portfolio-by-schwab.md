The **Balanced Tax-Aware Portfolio by Schwab** is a variation of Schwab's standard balanced model, adjusted to improve after-tax returns for investors holding assets in taxable brokerage accounts. It maintains a comparable equity-to-bond split to the standard balanced portfolio but substitutes tax-exempt municipal bonds for taxable bonds and favors equity funds with lower turnover and dividend distributions. Charles Schwab publishes these model portfolios as part of its broader investor education and guidance resources.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nThe portfolio applies the same core logic as the standard balanced allocation — broad diversification across stocks and bonds — but layers in tax efficiency as a primary design constraint. Municipal bonds replace taxable bonds to reduce ordinary income, and equity allocations are tilted toward investments that minimize taxable distributions. The premise is that for investors in higher tax brackets, what you keep after taxes matters as much as gross returns.\n\n## Who It's For\nThis portfolio is aimed at investors in mid-to-high marginal tax brackets who are investing through taxable accounts and want a balanced risk profile. It is less relevant for tax-advantaged accounts such as IRAs or 401(k)s, where the tax-aware adjustments provide no additional benefit.\n\n## Pros\n- Designed specifically to reduce the tax drag that erodes returns in taxable accounts\n- Municipal bond allocation can meaningfully improve after-tax yield for investors in higher brackets\n- Retains the simplicity and low-cost structure of a standard index-based balanced portfolio\n\n## Cons\n- Municipal bonds typically offer lower pre-tax yields, which can disadvantage investors in lower tax brackets\n- Tax-efficient equity funds may underweight certain sectors or factors, creating mild style drift\n- Tax-aware substitutions add complexity compared to a straightforward balanced portfolio\n\n## Technical Notes\nThe appropriateness of municipal bonds depends heavily on an investor's effective marginal tax rate. Investors should calculate their tax-equivalent yield before assuming the tax-aware version is superior to the standard balanced portfolio for their situation.
