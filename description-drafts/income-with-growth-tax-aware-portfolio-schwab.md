The **Income with Growth Tax-Aware Portfolio by Schwab** is the taxable-account variant of Schwab's income-with-growth model. It maintains the same general objective — producing income while allowing for some portfolio growth — but substitutes tax-exempt municipal bonds for taxable bonds and favours equity holdings that generate lower taxable distributions. Charles Schwab publishes this alongside its standard income-with-growth portfolio to serve investors who hold a significant portion of their assets in taxable brokerage accounts.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nThe underlying philosophy mirrors the standard income-with-growth model: balance current income needs against long-run purchasing power preservation. The tax-aware layer adds the constraint that the portfolio should minimise the tax cost of its income and capital gains. By favouring municipal bonds, the portfolio generates interest income that is generally exempt from federal income tax — and often state tax for in-state bonds — improving after-tax yield for investors in higher brackets.\n\n## Who It's For\nThis portfolio is most appropriate for investors in the mid-to-high marginal tax brackets who are drawing income from a taxable account and want to reduce their annual tax bill. It is less useful for assets held in tax-advantaged accounts such as IRAs, where the tax-exempt nature of municipal bond income provides no incremental benefit.\n\n## Pros\n- Municipal bond allocation generates tax-exempt income, improving after-tax yield for higher-bracket investors\n- Growth-oriented equity sleeve helps offset inflation over time\n- Low-cost index construction keeps total expenses low\n\n## Cons\n- Municipal bonds typically carry lower pre-tax yields, reducing gross income relative to the standard income-with-growth portfolio\n- Tax benefits diminish for investors in lower tax brackets, making the standard portfolio potentially superior for them\n- Rising interest rates still expose the bond sleeve to capital value risk regardless of tax status\n\n## Technical Notes\nInvestors should compare the after-tax yield of municipal bonds against taxable bonds at their specific marginal rate to determine whether the tax-aware portfolio is advantageous for their individual situation.
