The **Gone Fishin' Portfolio** was created by Alexander Green, investment director at The Oxford Club, and introduced in his 2008 book [*The Gone Fishin' Portfolio: Get Wise, Get Wealthy... and Get on With Your Life*](https://www.amazon.com/Gone-Fishin-Portfolio-Wise-Wealthy/dp/0470598190). Green designed the portfolio to require minimal ongoing attention -- the name deliberately evokes the idea that once you build it and commit to annual rebalancing, you can step away and live your life rather than obsessing over markets.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nGreen's strategy draws on the academic evidence supporting low-cost index funds and broad diversification across asset classes that respond differently to economic conditions. The portfolio holds a fixed mix of domestic and international equity funds at various market-cap and style exposures alongside bonds, real estate, precious metals, and natural resources. Annual rebalancing restores the target weights, which enforces a disciplined buy-low, sell-high dynamic without requiring any judgment about which assets will outperform. The philosophy holds that most individual investors destroy wealth through overtrading, high fees, and emotional reactions to short-term market moves -- and that a simple, diversified, low-cost portfolio beats complexity over time.\n\n## Who It's For\nThis portfolio suits investors who want a complete, globally diversified allocation that requires only one tune-up per year. The inclusion of precious metals and natural resources makes it more resilient to inflationary environments than typical domestic-only portfolios. It is appropriate for long time horizons and investors who want meaningful real asset exposure alongside traditional stocks and bonds.\n\n## Pros\n- Simple annual rebalancing discipline -- minimal ongoing time or attention required\n- Broad diversification includes real assets that can perform well during inflationary periods\n- Designed specifically for individual investors without access to institutional resources\n- Disciplined buy-and-hold approach reduces behavioral mistakes from overtrading\n\n## Cons\n- Relatively complex structure with allocations across multiple niche asset classes for a passive approach\n- Commodities and precious metals have underperformed stocks over very long periods, creating a return drag compared to equity-heavy portfolios\n- Annual rebalancing across many positions generates transaction costs and potential tax events in taxable accounts
