The **Mid-Twenties Portfolio** is the most aggressive of Burton Malkiel's age-based model allocations, presented in [*A Random Walk Down Wall Street*](https://www.amazon.com/Random-Walk-Down-Wall-Street/dp/0393358380). Malkiel, a Princeton economics professor and influential advocate of index investing, designed this allocation for young investors in their mid-twenties who have a very long time horizon ahead of them and the greatest capacity to absorb short-term portfolio losses. The allocation is heavily weighted toward equities, reflecting the mathematical reality that a young investor who stays invested through market cycles stands to benefit enormously from compounding.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nFor investors in their mid-twenties, Malkiel recommends a portfolio that is almost entirely in equities, with a very small or negligible bond allocation. The rationale is straightforward: with thirty or more years until retirement, the risk of a prolonged equity market downturn is manageable because time allows for recovery. The risk Malkiel views as most dangerous for young investors is not short-term volatility but the long-run cost of being too conservative and missing out on decades of equity returns.\n\n## Who It's For\nThis portfolio is designed specifically for investors in their mid-twenties with a retirement time horizon of roughly thirty or more years. It suits those with stable employment, limited near-term liquidity needs, and the emotional fortitude to watch their portfolio decline significantly without selling. It is not appropriate for investors who cannot stomach large drawdowns, regardless of age.\n\n## Pros\n- Maximum equity exposure captures the full benefit of long-run equity returns and compound growth\n- Diversification across domestic and international stocks reduces single-market concentration risk\n- Simple and low-cost when implemented with broad index funds\n\n## Cons\n- Very limited or no bond allocation provides almost no cushion during severe equity bear markets\n- Young investors with limited savings may find large percentage drawdowns psychologically difficult to endure\n- Generic age-based guidance does not account for individual circumstances such as job security or near-term financial goals
