**The Ben Felix Model Portfolio -- Timing** applies a trend-following overlay to the [Ben Felix Model Portfolio](/portfolios/ben-felix-model-portfolio), a globally diversified, factor-tilted equity strategy developed by [Ben Felix](https://pwlcapital.com/white-papers-five-factor-investing-with-etfs/) of PWL Capital. Each of the portfolio's six sleeves -- US large cap, US total market, international developed, US small cap value, emerging markets, and international small cap value -- is held at its target weight when its price is above its 10-month simple moving average, and replaced with short-term treasuries (cash) when it falls below. The core weights are identical to the buy-and-hold version: 30% US large cap, 30% US total market, 16% international developed, 10% US small cap value, 8% emerging markets, and 6% international small cap value.\n\n## Investment Philosophy\nThe strategy combines two distinct ideas: factor diversification (from Ben Felix's five-factor framework) and trend following (the 10-month SMA rule popularized by Meb Faber in [A Quantitative Approach to Tactical Asset Allocation](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=962461)). The premise is that factor tilts improve expected returns over full market cycles, while the SMA filter reduces exposure to sustained downtrends -- particularly useful for higher-volatility sleeves like emerging markets and international small cap value. The portfolio maintains the same factor exposure as the buy-and-hold version during bull markets, while rotating partially or fully to cash during sharp or prolonged drawdowns.\n\n## Who It's For\nSuited to investors who share the intellectual case for factor investing but want some protection against severe bear markets. The strategy requires more tolerance for tracking error than either a plain global index or the buy-and-hold Ben Felix portfolio -- the timing overlay will sometimes be in cash when the market is rising, and sometimes still invested during short, sharp corrections that don't persist long enough to trigger a signal. Minimum recommended time horizon is five years.\n\n## Pros\n- Combines factor diversification with systematic trend-following -- two historically independent sources of return improvement\n- The timing filter is simple and rules-based, with no discretion in execution\n- Has historically reduced maximum drawdown significantly relative to the buy-and-hold version\n\n## Cons\n- The SMA filter adds whipsaw risk: brief corrections can trigger a move to cash just before a rebound\n- Factor premiums are long-run phenomena; the timing overlay changes the holding-period return profile in ways that can underperform over shorter windows\n- Higher implementation complexity than the buy-and-hold version -- requires monthly attention to check and rebalance each sleeve independently
